**Why Regression on Damping Curves — When Inference Sees Only the Scan**

**A natural objection:** the final task is to detect defective units from the scan alone, since not all units go to the test rig. How does regression on damping curves help?

**Curves are needed only for training, never for inference**

The regression model learns the mapping scan → predicted damping force. In production it receives only a scan and returns a predicted value (or a full curve). The test rig is needed solely to produce training labels — exactly as with pass/fail. The only difference is how rich a label each tested unit provides. Classification then comes for free: since the specification thresholds are known (e.g., damping force must lie within X–Y at a given velocity), a predicted value outside the specification band → unit flagged as suspect.

**The final product is therefore exactly what the Customer wants — a defect detector operating on scans — with a regressor inside instead of a classifier.**

Why this beats training a classifier directly
1.	**Every unit carries information, not only defective ones.** With binary pass/fail, 490 good units tell the model the same thing over and over; it effectively learns only from ~10 defects. With regression, each of the 500 units has a different damping value — a unit in the middle of the specification band and a unit just at the threshold are two distinct, informative examples. Roughly 50× more signal is extracted from the same dataset.
2.	**The model learns a gradient of “how close to failure”, not a hard boundary.** Damping defects are almost never binary phenomena — units drift toward the threshold until they cross it. A classifier with 10 defects must guess where the boundary lies in geometry space with 10 points on one side; a regressor learns the full geometry→damping relationship, and the boundary follows naturally. This is the same property that makes regression viable from ~300–500 units while classification needs hundreds of defects.
3.	**The output is a margin, not just a verdict — operational gold.** The regressor output is a distance from the threshold, enabling a three-state policy: predicted value deep in spec → pass; close to the threshold (buffer zone) → route to the test rig; out of spec → hold / route to the rig. The model becomes a test-selection mechanism: instead of a random sample, the rig receives the units the model is least certain about or that sit closest to the threshold — raising detection at the same rig throughput. Bonus: units sent for testing return with true labels, so the system generates its own most valuable training data (de facto active learning), a loop that improves the model over time at no extra cost.
4.	**Thresholds can change without retraining.** Specifications may differ per product variant or per end customer. A pass/fail classifier has the threshold baked into its training labels — a specification change means relabeling and retraining. A regressor predicts a physical quantity; a new threshold is a one-line change in decision logic. Likewise, the buffer zone (sensitivity vs. false-alarm volume) is tuned on the business side without touching the model.
5.	**Diagnostics and engineer trust.** “The model predicts damping force 1,180 N against a specification of 1,200–1,400 N” is a statement a quality engineer can work with — confront it with the rig, see the error in newtons, assess calibration. “The model says NOK with probability 0.73” is much harder to validate and inspires less trust. At Phase 2 acceptance (“technical validation”), MAE in physical units is far easier to defend than classification metrics computed on ~3 defects in the test set.

**Honest caveats**
*	**The fundamental condition still applies:** geometry must carry signal about damping. Regression cannot conjure information absent from the scan — but it verifies the question faster and more cheaply: correlation of predictions with the rig (or lack thereof) is visible on a few hundred units, whereas a classifier on 10 defects gives no statistically credible answer in either direction.
*	**Non-damping defects need the second path.** Regression captures what manifests in damping force. Purely geometric/cosmetic defects, or issues manifesting as leaks or noise, are the domain of the anomaly detection path — hence the hybrid recommendation stands: regressor + geometric anomaly detector as two complementary sensors.
*	**Regressor error at the threshold must be quantified.** If model MAE is ±80 N and the specification band is 200 N wide, the buffer zone must be sized accordingly — and honestly compared against the rig's own repeatability (gauge R&R; if the rig itself scatters ±50 N, that is the natural ceiling on model accuracy).

**One-sentence version for the meeting:** “The regressor learns to predict the rig result from the scan; in production the rig is no longer needed — predictions are compared against specification thresholds, and units in the uncertainty zone are routed to a physical test. Every tested unit, not only defective ones, teaches the model — which at 500 units is the difference between a feasible and an infeasible project.”
